Tiles

Since the normal Settlers of Catan game is made out of cardboard with plastic game pieces, it can get quite messy as the game is played because the pieced are set at the intersections of the cardboard. I wanted to make sure my game had dedicated places for all the game tokens, ensuring that they would be neatly placed as the game is played.

To test out my whole process, I decided to make the simplest tile first: The void of space. Making this one would allow my to make sure that the magnet placement and tolerances once coated in XTC-3D were correct. I decided that these pieces, as they are not really necessary for the game, could be completely flat. I want to make little "galaxies" inside of them with the resin, which I can play around with once I have the molds.

Creating the simple tile in rhino was straightforward. I created and extruded a hexagon, and then used the ArrayPolar command to add other tile features:

Here, you can see that there are two holes on each hex for magnets (more on that later). I also added semi-circular indentations for the cities and settlements, and chamfered edges for the roads to sit in.

Resin Casting

I wanted to go with a galaxy color scheme. The four player colors will be purple, blue, green, and pink. After setting up my work area (complete with a fan to blow away the fumes) I did an initial test with some of my mica powders in the token mold:

Mixing the mica powders was very fun to watch! They swirl around like pearlescent galaxies while you stir them. The photos don't do it justice. I also found that a tiny bit of mica powder goes a very long way. After filling the tokens, I filled my hex mold:

I had a bit of bubble action in my hex mold. I was able to pop the large bubbles with a toothpick, but most of them went away slowly as it cured.

I also coated my base molds in XTC-3D. I haven'y been able to cast silicone in them yet to see how that will turn out, but they are definitely smoother with the coating.

I pulled out my Kaonashi mold from the previous project and put some leftover resin in it. The striations are very clear, so when I was coating my other molds in XTC-3D I also redid the Kaonashi mold. Now, I can make another Kaonashi that will be much smoother.

Silicone tools

When I was buying the resin online, I saw some reusable silicone cups in the recommendations list. These turned out to be a really great purchase! Once you're finished mixing, you just let the leftover resin cure in the silicone cup and remove it. Voila, the cup is ready to use again! No more disposable plastic! I also found that the silicone cups were very easy to mix in because they were flexible and completely smooth on the inside, so my mixing stick could easily scrape every part of the cup, unlike the plastic cups I used for mixing plaster and Oomoo which have grooves where bits of unmixed material remains. I liked these again, so I also ordered some little ones to use for mixing colors.

Adding Magnets

I chose to use two magnets in each side of the hex piece because: 1) I would have a stronger connection between each piece, and 2) I wouldn't have to worry about polarity issues. By making the right magnet always North out and the left always South out, every hex will be able to connect to every other hex.

For my first test, I demolded my hex tile when it was still a bit flexible. This allowed me to push in the magnets into the holes with a bit of give. I haven't been able to test how well they stay in the holes, and I may need to superglue them. One other idea is to coat the whole tile in XTC-3D after the magnets are in place, which would give it a smooth surface and hold the magnet in.
